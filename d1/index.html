01 The Problem

You've spent decades perfecting what you make.
You've spent almost nothing on what it means.

The RFPs went to the company with the better story. The talent chose the employer with the clearer mission. The investors backed the team that could explain what they do in one sentence.

2024
Brand Architecture

Edge AI Storage Leader

They were the best-kept secret in edge AI. A storage component vendor with technology that powered autonomous vehicles, smart factories, space missions — and a brand that said none of it. We rebuilt their entire narrative around one idea: they don't sell storage, they architect intelligence. Within a year, tier-one partnerships with the world's largest chip platforms.

→ This project taught me how to translate deep-tech positioning — a pattern I'd use again across industrial computing and semiconductor branding.
2023
Brand Narrative

Precision Test & Measurement

They build precision test instruments that validate everything from EV batteries to 5G chips. Their data sheets were flawless. Their story was invisible. I spent three weeks in their labs, watching engineers test components that would end up in products used by billions. The brand narrative I built didn't simplify what they do — it elevated it.

→ Understanding precision measurement gave me the language for semiconductor branding — directly useful when the next chipmaker came calling.
2024
Employer Brand Strategy

Global Semiconductor Giant

Their chips power 2 billion devices a year, but in the US and India talent markets, they were losing engineers to companies with louder brands. The challenge wasn't awareness — it was perception. I built an employer brand strategy that reframed their mission: not just making chips, but democratizing the technology that shapes daily life for billions of people who'll never know their name.

04 Origin

Process engineer.
Brand strategist.
Systems architect.

I started my career designing process systems for power plants and EV charging infrastructure. I learned something that never left me: the most sophisticated systems in the world fail if the people operating them don't understand why they matter.

That realization pulled me into brand strategy. For fifteen years, I've worked at the intersection of technology and meaning — helping leaders across semiconductor fabrication, chip design, edge AI, and industrial computing close the gap between what they build and what the world understands.

The honest part: the first three years were humbling. I came from engineering where problems had solutions. Brand strategy has no right answers — only better questions. I failed spectacularly before I found my footing. That failure taught me more than any success: systems break. What matters is whether they break forward.

Today, as Creative Intelligence Officer at RedPeak, part of the kyu Collective — I lead strategic engagements across Asia's technology sector. My approach is shaped by an engineer's insistence on systems, a strategist's instinct for narrative, and a musician's ear for resonance.

When I'm not building brand systems, I play handpan — a steel instrument tuned to specific harmonic frequencies. Every note resonates with every other note. Nothing is random. That's how I think about brands, too.
